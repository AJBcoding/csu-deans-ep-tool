M03 — How the benchmark is selected

How the rule routes a program to the figure your graduates' median earnings get compared against, and why graduate programs are scored against the lowest of three numbers.

The short version

The earnings test compares two numbers: your program's median earnings (graduates who are working and not still in school) and a benchmark drawn from federal Census-derived data. The institution does not pick the benchmark. The rule routes each program to a specific benchmark depending on credential level and where the institution's students live.

For undergraduate programs, the benchmark is what working adults earn with a high-school credential and no college degree. For graduate and professional programs, the benchmark is the lowest of three figures drawn from working adults with a baccalaureate. The lowest-of-three rule is the one that decides whether a graduate program clears or fails by a comfortable margin.

Undergraduate programs — high-school benchmark

For programs at the bachelor's, associate's, or undergraduate certificate level, the benchmark is the median earnings of working adults aged 25 to 34 with a high-school credential and no postsecondary degree. The figure is drawn from federal Census earnings data for the state where the institution is located.

There is one routing rule on top of that. If fewer than 50 percent of the institution's enrolled students are residents of that state, the comparison shifts to the national figure rather than the state figure. The proposed rule writes this skip-calculation rule into § 668.402(c)(3): the in-state share is read from the institution's own enrollment file (the field is published as a fall-2018 / fall-2019 in-state enrollment share), and a share below 50 percent triggers the national fallback.

For most public institutions whose students live in-state, the state figure applies. For institutions with majority out-of-state enrollment, the comparison shifts to the national figure — which is generally higher than any single state figure, so the benchmark moves up.

Graduate programs — the lowest of three

For graduate, professional, and graduate-certificate programs, the rule constructs the benchmark by reading three numbers and taking whichever is lowest. The three numbers are drawn from federal Census earnings data for working adults with a baccalaureate degree:

Figure 1 — State, any field Median earnings of working adults with a baccalaureate, in the state where the institution is located, across all fields of study. This is the broadest of the three.
Figure 2 — State, same field Median earnings of working adults with a baccalaureate in the same field of study (matched at the 2-digit federal CIP code level), in the state where the institution is located.
Figure 3 — National, same field Median earnings of working adults with a baccalaureate in the same field of study, nationally. This is the federal-level fallback for fields whose earnings data does not survive a state-level population threshold.

The benchmark for a graduate program is the lowest of those three figures. The lowest-of-three rule means that a graduate program in a low-paying baccalaureate field is compared against a benchmark drawn from that low field, not against the broader baccalaureate population.

A practical example: a Music master's program in California is measured against three numbers — the California baccalaureate median across all fields, the California-baccalaureate-in-arts median, and the national-baccalaureate-in-arts median — and the rule picks whichever is lowest. The California-arts figure is typically the lowest of the three, because arts baccalaureates earn less than the broader baccalaureate population in California. A Music master's program is therefore being compared against working adults who earned a baccalaureate in arts and went into the workforce.

Why "lowest of three" matters

The lowest-of-three rule was a substantive choice the Department made on top of the underlying statute. The statutory text in OBBBA § 84001(c)(3)(B) defines the candidate figures; the procedural choice to pick the lowest of them is what gives many arts and humanities graduate programs the smallest benchmark to clear. A program at the master's level is measured against baccalaureate-holders in the same broad field. The reading "if a baccalaureate in your field earns this much, your master's should clear that figure" is the policy-level argument the rule rests on.

The lowest-of-three rule is also why graduate programs in higher-paying fields rarely fail the test. A graduate program in engineering is measured against engineering baccalaureates — and engineering baccalaureates earn enough that the lowest of the three figures is itself high. The same procedural choice routes arts and humanities graduate programs to a low benchmark and engineering graduate programs to a high one.

If the benchmark population at the state-level field-of-study cell contains fewer than 30 working adults at the required credential, a benchmark-side expansion fires independently of the program's own cohort expansion (M01). The two expansions are independent machinery; either or both can fire on a single program.

What this means in practice

For a graduate program in arts or humanities, the benchmark is almost always the field-restricted state figure. A pass-or-fail margin of a few thousand dollars is common, because the figure your program is compared against is itself a low-paying-field number.

For an undergraduate program, the benchmark is a high-school number. Bachelor's degrees in most fields clear that benchmark comfortably; the failures concentrated in the federal data file are at the graduate level for that reason.

The benchmark routing happens deterministically at federal-data build time. The institution does not see a choice of benchmarks; it sees one figure published alongside the program's own median. The tool surfaces both sides of the comparison and the gap percentage between them.
