M01 — How a program is counted

The cohort floor and what happens when the Department has already published a verdict for your program.

● Public data only · No private or institutional data leaves your browser

The short version

The earnings test only fires on a program once enough graduates are in the measured group. The federal rule sets a floor of 30 graduates. If a single award year does not reach that floor, the rule expands the count — first by pooling more years, then by pooling related programs at wider levels of the federal CIP coding system. If the count never reaches 30, the program is reported as NOT MEASURED for that year.

On top of that floor, the Department has already published a pre-computed pass/fail flag for many programs in its public data file. When that flag is present, the tool surfaces it as the authoritative verdict. Local re-derivation only fills in where the Department has left a blank.

The four-step expansion

The rule asks four questions in order. Each step is taken only if the previous step's count is still under 30 graduates.

Step 1 Single-year cohort at the program's own specific code (the full six-digit federal CIP). If 30 or more graduates show up in that single award year, the rule stops here — the program is measured at its own median.
Step 2 Five award years pooled at the program's own specific code. The rule pools the most-recent five years rather than picking one. The published median is the median of that pooled population.
Step 3 Five years at the program's specific code, plus related programs at the 4-digit family — programs that share the first four digits of the federal CIP code. The 4-digit family captures programs the Department treats as substantively similar.
Step 4 Five years at the specific code, plus the 4-digit family, plus related programs at the wider 2-digit family — the broadest program grouping the federal CIP code defines. If the count is still under 30 after this step, no median is published.

Each step adds graduates from the wider program group; it does not discard the inner group. The expansion only stops when 30 is reached or when there are no wider groups left to pool.

The rule's preamble describes this expansion as moving "in order to achieve a cohort of at least 30 individuals." The four-step structure is the proposed rule's procedural answer to the statutory floor written into OBBBA itself.

The published-flag override

The rule does not run live on your data. The Department publishes a federal data file each release cycle that already includes a pre-computed pass/fail flag for the programs it can score. The field name is fail_obbb_cip2_wageb: a value of 1 means fail under the rule's published verdict, 0 means pass, and a blank means no published verdict.

When that field is present and non-blank, this tool treats it as authoritative. The on-screen verdict reads from the federal file directly. Local re-computation against median earnings and the benchmark figure runs in parallel as a cross-check, but it does not replace the published value.

Local re-derivation only becomes the surfaced verdict when the Department has not published a value for that program — which mostly happens for rows the rule reports as NOT MEASURED for an unrelated reason (cohort below floor, fewer than 16 IRS records, or out-of-scope credential level).

If the tool's local re-derivation disagrees with the Department's published verdict, the on-screen card surfaces both side by side and flags the disagreement. Disagreement is rare; it points to a real-world data edge worth verifying against the federal source before any external use.

What this means in practice

For a small program at a single institution, the published verdict is most often the binding answer. If the program reaches the floor on its own cohort or a multi-year pool, the score is its own median. If the program is small enough that the rule has to pool related programs, the score is a wider-group median — and a small program can pass or fail on a number that is not really its own.

When the count never reaches 30 and no wider grouping closes the gap, the program is reported NOT MEASURED. NOT MEASURED is not a pass and not a fail. The rule's program-level Title IV consequence (M05) does not attach to a NOT MEASURED program.

The expansion described here is independent of the rule's benchmark expansion (M03). Both can fire, and they can pull graduates from different groupings on each side of the comparison.
