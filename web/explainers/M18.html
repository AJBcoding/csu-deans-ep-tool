M18 — When program failure could spread to the institution

Proposed § 668.16(t) is the rule's bridge from a program-level finding to an institution-level Title IV finding. Five independent legal frames each give that bridge a separate question to answer.

The short version

The OBBBA earnings-premium statute is written program by program. A failing program loses Direct Loan eligibility under the program-level mechanism (M05). Proposed 34 CFR § 668.16(t) is the rule's separate machinery for letting program-level failures escalate into an institution-level Title IV finding — meaning the institution itself, not just one program, could be found out of compliance.

The proposed mechanism uses share thresholds applied across two of three measurement years. Phase 2 of the project's audit walked the same proposal through five independent legal registers. Each register raises a separate, independently sufficient question about the proposed § 668.16(t) design. None of the five is by itself a verdict; together they describe a convergence — a situation in which any one of five doctrinal hooks could carry a stakeholder challenge.

Frame 1 — Statutory authority (Loper Bright + major-questions)

OBBBA § 84001 sets the program-level earnings test. The proposed rule points to § 498 of the Higher Education Act for the institution-level escalation pathway. After Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024), courts read agency statutes themselves rather than defer to the agency's reading. The major-questions canon adds skepticism whenever an agency claims an unusually large or politically significant new power inside a smaller statutory authority.

The unanswered question this frame raises: does a program-level earnings statute, operating through an administrative-capability provision in a separate statute, carry enough textual authority to support an institution-level Title IV consequence the underlying earnings statute does not itself name? The proposed rule's text alone does not settle that question.

Frame 2 — Problem statement (OIRA Circular A-4 § 1)

OMB Circular A-4 — the federal rulemaking handbook for regulatory analysis — opens by asking each rule to state the problem it solves. The preamble's discussion of the institution-level escalation pathway uses words like "would suggest" and "could represent": framings that point at a possible problem rather than demonstrating one.

A-4 § 1 expects a demonstrated problem, not an asserted one — empirical evidence that the institution-level escalation pathway addresses a real and otherwise-unaddressed harm. As of the published preamble, the record does not show that demonstration. That gap is by itself an A-4 deficiency a public comment can surface.

Frame 3 — Alternatives review (OIRA Circular A-4 § 6)

A-4 § 6 expects the preamble to consider serious alternatives to the chosen design and to analyze each at comparable depth. The preamble does mention alternatives — disclosure-only, threshold variations, phase-in approaches — but it does not provide comparable benefit-cost tables for any of them.

The unanswered question this frame raises: were the alternatives actually considered, in the A-4 sense, or only narrated? The difference matters. If a court reviewing the rule finds that the chosen design and its alternatives received different levels of analytical rigor, the alternatives-review frame becomes a freestanding hook for a remand under the Administrative Procedure Act.

Frame 4 — Uncertainty (OIRA Circular A-4 § E)

The proposed § 668.16(t) design rests on two procedural choices the rule does not vary: a 50-percent share threshold, and a two-failures-in-three-years window. A-4 § E expects the preamble to publish sensitivity analysis around the choices that swing the rule's outcome.

As written, the preamble does not publish bounds for either choice. It does not analyze the published-flag-publication gap (cells whose pool-level fail flag the Department has chosen not to publish, leaving cohorts measurable but verdicts blanked). A-4 § E asks for that analysis on the record. Its absence is a third independently sufficient frame for stakeholder challenge.

Frame 5 — Reasoned decisionmaking (State Farm)

Motor Vehicle Manufacturers Association v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co., 463 U.S. 29 (1983), is the case courts cite when reviewing whether an agency's decision is "arbitrary and capricious." It identifies four prongs the agency must satisfy: relevant data considered, satisfactory explanation given, judgment supported by the record, and alternatives addressed.

The institution-level escalation pathway as published does not, on its face, meet all four prongs at the depth State Farm expects. Whether a reviewing court would so conclude is an unanswered question — but the four prongs are the floor an agency must clear. Independent of the four A-4 frames, State Farm gives a stakeholder challenge a fifth doctrinal hook.

What "convergence" means here

The five frames are not a stacked argument. They are five independent doctrinal pathways, each grounded in different legal source material — one statutory-interpretation frame, three OMB regulatory-analysis frames, one judicial-review frame. The convergence is the observation that the same proposed mechanism has at least one open question on every one of those five frames.

For a dean reading this, the operational read is conservative: if a program at this institution fails the earnings test in the first measurement year, the program-level Title IV consequences (M05, M07) attach to that program. The institution-level § 668.16(t) escalation pathway is separate machinery whose legal status is itself contested on five independent fronts. A public comment, a stakeholder filing, or a reviewing court could surface any one of these frames as a basis for challenging the institution-level mechanism without reaching the others.

This panel exists to surface the load-bearing legal question: whether the proposed rule, as written, can support the institution-level escalation given that the underlying earnings-premium statute is written program by program.
