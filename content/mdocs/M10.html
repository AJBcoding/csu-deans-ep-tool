M10

Authority architecture and severability. The federal rule sits on three statutory pillars (OBBBA § 84001, HEA § 454 as amended, GEPA § 431) with severability clauses at the rule's federal-loan-eligibility, disclosure, and admin-capability layers.

Litigation posture matters because a court order that invalidates one piece of the rule does not automatically invalidate the rest. The rule's severability architecture and its three-layer statutory authority structure are the constraints on what a successful challenge can actually achieve.

How it works

  • Statutory pillars. OBBBA § 84001 amends HEA § 454 to create the earnings-premium ineligibility regime. The Secretary's authority for the proposed regulations runs through HEA § 454 (post-amendment), the Secretary's general HEA rulemaking authority, and GEPA § 431 (the master-calendar-waiver authority the Department invoked for the rulemaking schedule).
  • Severability clauses. The proposed rule includes severability language at three locations corresponding to the rule's distinct consequences: federal-loan-eligibility loss, disclosure obligations, and the admin-capability gate. A challenge that succeeds at one layer does not necessarily reach the others.
  • Master-calendar-waiver. The Department waived the negotiated-rulemaking master calendar to issue the proposed rule on its accelerated schedule. Litigation challenging the waiver itself is procedural rather than substantive; a successful waiver challenge could delay the rule but not invalidate § 84001 (which is statutory, not regulatory).
  • OBBBA § 84001 itself. The statute is self-executing on the PPA-assurance side: even if the proposed regulations were enjoined, the statutory framework remains in effect from July 1, 2026 forward. The Department would still be required to implement some regulation; the proposed text just would not be the operative one.

What this means for planning horizons: a regulatory challenge that succeeds in pushing the proposed rule back to negotiated rulemaking buys time on the consequence schedule (M13), not relief from the underlying statutory regime.
