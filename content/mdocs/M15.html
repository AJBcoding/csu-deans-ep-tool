M15

Strategic response options and what the federal rule does to each. Merge, restructure, credential-shift, and CIP-recoding moves all interact with the cohort-construction and benchmark-construction logic — sometimes favorably, often not.

An institution facing program-level exposure has several structural options: merge programs into a wider department, change a program's CIP code, shift a credential up or down, restructure as a graduate certificate, or close the program. Each option interacts with the rule differently; some changes the cohort, some changes the benchmark, and some changes neither.

How it works

  • Merge into a wider CIP family. A merge that crosses 4-digit CIP boundaries changes which program the rule treats as the unit of measurement. If the merged program now occupies a higher-earning CIP family on average, the benchmark may shift unfavorably (M03). The cohort itself does not necessarily grow: the rule already pools across the 4-digit family at expansion step three (M01).
  • CIP recoding without merger. Reassigning a program to a different 6-digit CIP changes the program's identity in federal data. The proposed § 600.21(a)(11) reporting requirement makes this a tracked institutional event (M11). Recoding does not retroactively change historical cohorts.
  • Credential shift (BA → certificate, MFA → master's). A change in credential level can move the program out of one rule-scoped population into another (or out of scope entirely; M12). The benchmark population shifts correspondingly.
  • Closure and orderly wind-down. Proposed § 668.603(c)(4) governs orderly closure. A closed program produces no future cohorts; existing borrowers are protected under the wind-down window (M05).
  • What does not change. Strategic moves do not change the Department's empirical record on named populations (M14). A CIP family the Department flagged as elevated-risk remains so regardless of one institution's reorganization.

This panel does not prescribe. It surfaces the structural mechanics so a Dean's planning conversation runs against an accurate model of what each option actually does to the rule's input variables.
